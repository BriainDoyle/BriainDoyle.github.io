High-Speed PCR

Accelerating DNA analysis with Lab-on-a-Disc technology.

Type Masters Thesis
Timeline 2025 - Present
Role Lead Researcher

The Mission

The goal is to create a proof of concept for high-speed Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) on the Lab-on-a-Disc (LoaD) architecture.

  • Continuous Flow: Remove the need for thermal ramping by utilising the centrifugal forces from spinning the disc to precisely move the sample through fixed thermal zones.
  • PoC Optimisation: Design for Point of Care, constraining the system to a ~10W power draw and a portable footprint.

Design & Iteration

Microfluidic Disc Design

I engineered and fabricated multi-layer microfluidic discs using laser-cut PMMA and PSA. By designing serpentine channels within the PSA layers, they were confined to a height of 86µm, allowing for a high surface area to volume ratio. The serpentine channels spanned the disc radially to allow for the centrifugal forces to aid in moving the sample through the channels and to ensure the sample entered the two fixed thermal zones every cycle.

Heating Elements Construction

I designed and assembled a dual-sided heating system featuring four ring shaped thermal zones to ensure uniform temperature distribution across the multi-layered disc. By incorporating wound NiCr wire with anodised aluminium heat spreaders, I achieved stable denaturation (95°C) and annealing/extension (60°C) zones through power efficient Joule heating. This fixed-temperature configuration removes the high-power requirements from rapid thermal cycling utilised in standard “Time Domain” PCR, while capitalising on the centrifugal forces of the spinning disc.

Automated Calculations

To manage the high precision required for microfluidics, I developed a comprehensive mathematical model in Excel to automate the calculation of volumetric flow rates, fluid residency times, and multi-modal thermal power losses. This tool enabled real-time analysis of the "knock-on" effects caused by adjusting rotational frequency or channel geometry, ensuring the system remained within the strict power budget.

Electronics Integration

I designed and fabricated a custom PCB "hat" to interface with the ESP-S3 on DCU’s ReLoaD wireless power platform, enabling the integration of control electronics directly onto the rotating disc. The board features a high-efficiency buck converter to manage power distribution for the heaters, alongside signal conditioning circuitry, including low-pass filters and voltage dividers, for precise thermistor-based temperature sensing. By implementing PWM controlled power transistors, I can achieve the closed-loop PID control necessary to maintain stable thermal zones within the system's strict wireless power budget.
